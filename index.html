"Is the most luminous known quasar at z > 6 actually as massive as it appears?"

Quasar J0100+2802 was estimated to host a black hole of ~12 billion solar masses, seemingly too large to have formed so early in cosmic history. Fujimoto et al. (2020) used ALMA submillimeter imaging to test whether gravitational lensing by a foreground galaxy was inflating the apparent brightness and inferred mass.

This simulation models the central mechanism of Fujimoto et al. (2020): the change in inferred black hole mass derived from applying a gravitational lens model to ALMA imaging. In the paper, ALMA resolves quasar J0100+2802 into four compact components - a signature of strong lensing. By fitting a point-mass lens model, Fujimoto shows that the apparent ~12 B M☉ black hole is magnified by a foreground galaxy, with the true mass falling below ~1 B M☉. The Einstein radius in this interactive scales to illustrate that relationship: as magnification increases, inferred brightness (and thus inferred mass) rises, while the true underlying source remains the same.

Inspired by Fujimoto's CGM research · Milky Way-like dark matter halo · Cold accretion → shock heating → hot halo → galactic fountain → repeat

PHASE 1 — COLD ACCRETION

Thick blue streams of cold gas (10⁴ K) flow directly into the galaxy from the cosmic web. No hot halo exists yet. Accretion is efficient and rapid, star formation is vigorous as raw fuel pours in. The galaxy core brightens as it builds stellar mass.

PHASE 2 — SHOCK HEATING

As the halo grows in mass, infalling gas is shock-heated at the virial radius. An orange-red hot atmosphere begins forming around the galaxy. Incoming streams slow, bend and diffuse. Some are stopped at the boundary entirely. Heat ripples propagate outward.

PHASE 3 — HOT HALO DOMINANCE

A large, diffuse hot CGM halo (10⁶ K) envelops the galaxy. Cold inflow is reduced to thin wisps. Inside the galaxy, supernova feedback drives energy into the surrounding medium. Motion becomes slow and floaty; the circumgalactic medium is now thermally dominated.

PHASE 4 — GALACTIC FOUNTAIN

Hot gas ejected by supernovae (orange arcs) rises outward, cools through purple into blue and falls back toward the galaxy as recycled cold inflow. This fountain cycle re-seeds star formation. The loop then restarts - connecting outflow back into the next accretion phase.

Biographical Information

Seiji Fujimoto is an assistant professor of astronomy at the University of Toronto whose research focuses on how the first galaxies and black holes formed in the early universe. (Fujimoto, 2025) He grew up near Kyoto, Japan, and was a competitive athlete before turning to science – playing baseball until elbow injuries ended his career at fifteen, after which he redirected that drive into Double Dutch and became a world champion in 2014 (Fujimoto, 2025). He earned his Bachelor's, Master's, and PhD in Astronomy from the University of Tokyo in 2019, then held postdoctoral positions as a DAWN Fellow in Copenhagen and a NASA Hubble Fellow in Austin before joining the University of Toronto as faculty in 2025 (Fujimoto, 2025).

What makes Fujimoto exceptional as a researcher is the remarkable volume and range of his output. As of 2025 he has published 18 first-authored papers – articles in which he personally led the scientific work – in peer-reviewed journals including Nature and Nature Astronomy, covering topics from the sizes of dusty star-forming galaxies (Fujimoto et al., 2018) to whether a record-breaking black hole might be an optical illusion of gravity (Fujimoto et al., 2020). That kind of sustained, first-authored productivity at an early career stage is rare and it reflects exactly the qualities his biography suggests: discipline, adaptability and the willingness to pursue difficult questions relentlessly.

Research

One of Fujimoto's most compelling first-authored papers investigates whether the most massive known black hole in the early universe is really what it appears to be; it is titled "Truth or Delusion? A Possible Gravitational Lensing Interpretation of the Ultraluminous Quasar SDSS J010013.02+280225.8 at z = 6.30" (Fujimoto et al., 2020). The object at the centre of the paper, a quasar called J0100+2802, is seen at a redshift of z = 6.30 – meaning we observe it as it existed less than one billion years after the Big Bang, a direct look back in time that connects to one of the central ideas in AST201. Quasars are powered by supermassive black holes actively consuming surrounding material, releasing energy so intense that a single quasar can outshine an entire galaxy; the AST201 AGN lecture explains this framework precisely (Hirsch, 2026, slides 8–11). Based on its observed brightness, earlier studies estimated J0100+2802's black hole at 12 billion solar masses, seemingly too large to have grown so quickly under any standard model.

Rather than accepting that estimate, Fujimoto asked whether gravitational lensing could be magnifying the quasar's light, making it appear far brighter than it truly is (Fujimoto et al., 2020). Gravitational lensing – the bending of light by a massive foreground object – is a direct consequence of general relativity introduced in the course as a prediction of how mass warps spacetime (Hirsch, 2026, slides 39–44). Using high-resolution ALMA images, he found the quasar's dust emission split into four compact components, a classic lensing signature. Spectroscopic data confirmed a foreground galaxy along the same line of sight and a lens model showed it could plausibly produce exactly that four-image pattern. If correct, the true black hole mass falls below one billion solar masses. Still large, but no longer theoretically impossible.

Fujimoto acknowledges that the four components could alternatively represent dusty star-forming regions in an early merging system, a scenario that connects to our discussions of how galaxies grow through mergers and gas inflow. Presenting both possibilities honestly, rather than overstating one interpretation, is precisely the kind of careful scientific reasoning the paper models.
